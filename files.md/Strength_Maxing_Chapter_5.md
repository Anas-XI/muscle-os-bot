## Strength Maxing
## The Neurological Emperor

Strength is a skill. This is not a motivational reframe — it is a precise neurophysiological
statement that changes everything about how you should train for it. Maximal force production is
governed primarily by the central nervous system: how many motor units fire simultaneously,
how fast they fire, how well they coordinate, and how much protective inhibition the brain is
willing to release in a given moment. Muscle size contributes to strength — but it is the floor of
your potential, not the ceiling. The ceiling is set by neural efficiency. This chapter is about
raising that ceiling as high as biology and evidence allow.
-  Strength Is a Neurological Skill — The Fundamental
## Reframe
Consider the evidence that demolishes the 'bigger = stronger' equation as a complete model:
elite powerlifters and weightlifters routinely out-lift bodybuilders with significantly larger muscle
mass. A 90kg powerlifter can squat 300kg. A 120kg bodybuilder with 30% more muscle tissue
may squat 180kg. The difference is not muscle — it is neural efficiency, motor pattern precision,
and years of practising the specific skill of maximal force expression.
The neural adaptations that drive strength development operate on a fundamentally different
timeline and through different mechanisms than hypertrophy. Understanding this separation is
the prerequisite for programming that maximises both simultaneously — and for understanding
why a 12-week hypertrophy block and a 12-week strength peak require entirely different
approaches.
The Three Pillars of Neural Strength
- Motor unit synchronisation: The simultaneous firing of multiple motor units to produce
peak force in a single instant. Untrained individuals fire motor units asynchronously —
the nervous system staggers their activation as a fatigue-management strategy. Trained
strength athletes develop the ability to synchronise motor unit discharge, producing force
peaks that the asynchronous pattern cannot generate. This is a trainable adaptation that
improves specifically with maximal and near-maximal loading.
- Rate coding (firing rate): The frequency at which an activated motor unit fires. A motor
unit firing at 100 Hz produces dramatically more force than the same unit firing at 25 Hz
— due to the summation of calcium transients in the muscle fibre (temporal summation).
Elite strength athletes achieve higher peak firing rates through adaptation of the
descending motor pathways and reduced afterhyperpolarisation in alpha motor neurons.
This is the mechanism behind the 'twitch' feel of a perfectly executed maximal lift.

- Reduced central inhibition: The brain maintains protective inhibitory circuits that
prevent maximal voluntary muscle activation — a biological governor against
catastrophic tendon rupture or joint injury. Golgi tendon organs (GTOs), Ia inhibitory
interneurons, and descending cortical inhibition all limit force output below true
physiological maximum. Strength training progressively reduces this inhibitory threshold
— allowing more of the available motor unit pool to be voluntarily recruited. This is why
extreme emotional arousal, acute pain, or adrenaline surges can produce supra-maximal
force outputs (the 'adrenaline strength' of emergency situations) — the inhibitory system
is temporarily bypassed.

## THE STRENGTH CONTINUUM
Muscle size is the floor of your strength potential. Neural efficiency is the ceiling. A
hypertrophy block builds a bigger engine. A strength-specific block teaches the nervous
system to run that engine at full throttle. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.
This is why the optimal long-term strength programme alternates hypertrophy blocks
(building the muscle mass base) with intensification blocks (maximising the neural
expression of that mass). Attempting to maximise both simultaneously in every session is
the most common programming error among intermediate lifters — and the reason most of
them plateau after 2 years.

-  The Neuromuscular Cascade: What Happens in the
Second Before a Maximal Lift
A maximal strength attempt is a precisely orchestrated neurological event. Understanding the
cascade from intention to contraction reveals exactly which variables to train and which to stop
leaving to chance.
## Step 1 — Descending Motor Drive: The Brain's Command Signal
Voluntary force production begins in the primary motor cortex (M1), where pyramidal neurons
project descending signals through the corticospinal tract to alpha motor neurons in the spinal
cord. The amplitude of this descending drive — motor cortex excitability — determines how
many motor units are recruited and at what firing rate. Psychological states that increase motor
cortex excitability (high arousal, controlled aggression, pre-lift priming) measurably increase
voluntary force output without any change in muscle physiology. This is not psychology — it is
neuroscience with direct programming implications.
Step 2 — Spinal Processing: The Gate Between Intent and Output
Before the motor signal reaches muscle, it passes through spinal interneuron networks that
modulate it in both excitatory and inhibitory directions. Key players:
- Ia afferents (muscle spindles): Detect muscle stretch rate and length. During the
eccentric phase of a lift, rapid stretch of the muscle spindle generates an Ia afferent
signal that reflexively activates the same muscle (stretch reflex), contributing to the force
production at the bottom of a squat or bench press. Training to deliberately use this

stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) — via controlled eccentric speed followed by explosive
concentric intent — amplifies force output beyond what pure concentric strength allows.
- Golgi tendon organs (GTOs): Detect tension in the musculotendinous junction. At high
tension thresholds, GTOs trigger Ib afferent inhibition — the autogenic inhibition that
constitutes the biological 'governor.' Strength training progressively raises the tension
threshold at which GTOs trigger inhibition — directly expanding the safe operating range
of voluntary force expression.
- Renshaw cells: Inhibitory interneurons that provide recurrent inhibition of alpha motor
neurons, preventing runaway firing rates. With training, Renshaw cell inhibition is
modulated to permit higher sustained firing rates — contributing to the rate coding
adaptation described above.
Step 3 — Excitation-Contraction Coupling: The Muscle End
The motor neuron action potential triggers acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction,
depolarising the muscle fibre membrane and initiating calcium release from the sarcoplasmic
reticulum via the ryanodine receptor. Calcium binds troponin C, shifting tropomyosin to expose
actin-binding sites, allowing myosin cross-bridge formation and force production. The rate of
calcium transient rise and the density of myosin heavy chain isoforms (Type IIx > IIa > I for force
production speed) determine the speed and magnitude of force development. This is the
mechanical endpoint of the entire neural cascade — and it is optimised by both hypertrophy
(more contractile protein) and neural training (faster, more synchronised calcium release).

-  Rate of Force Development: The Variable Nobody in the
## Gym Talks About
Most gym-goers train for peak force — the maximum tension a muscle can develop given
unlimited time. But in every athletic movement that matters — a sprint start, a jump, a throw, a
tackle, a competition squat — the time available to develop force is brutally short: typically 50–
250 milliseconds. Peak force in a maximally contracted muscle requires 300–500 milliseconds
to develop. In sport, you rarely have that time.
Rate of Force Development (RFD) — the slope of the force-time curve, measured in Newtons
per second — is the variable that bridges gym strength and athletic performance. High RFD
means you can express a large fraction of your peak force in the short time windows available in
athletic movements. Low RFD means your gym numbers don't fully transfer to sport
performance.
The Physics of Impulse — Why Explosive Training Matters
The mechanical impulse applied to an object equals the change in momentum it produces:
Impulse = Force × Time = ΔMomentum. In a fixed-duration athletic movement (say, 200ms
contact time in a jump), the only way to increase impulse — and therefore jump height — is to
increase force production in that 200ms window. This is achieved not by increasing peak
strength per se, but by increasing the rate at which force rises in the early phase of contraction
(the first 100ms, dominated entirely by neural factors — firing rate, synchronisation, and initial
motor unit recruitment).

This is why Olympic weightlifters, despite often being smaller than powerlifters, can produce
extraordinary ground reaction forces in sport: they train specifically in the explosive velocity
range that develops early RFD. Their nervous systems are calibrated for instantaneous force —
a completely different neural quality from grinding a heavy deadlift.
Training RFD: Ballistic and Plyometric Methods
- Ballistic movements (intent-driven): Jump squats, medicine ball throws, trap bar
jumps, push-up clap variations. The defining feature is intent to project the load — the
nervous system recruits high-threshold units immediately because of the explosive intent
signal, even with sub-maximal loads. 3–5 sets of 3–6 reps, maximal intent, full recovery
(3–5 min) between sets. Programme in the early portion of a session before
accumulated fatigue impairs velocity expression.
- Olympic lift derivatives: Power cleans, hang power snatches, push jerks. These
movements require rapid triple extension (ankle, knee, hip) with near-maximal
acceleration — producing the highest ground reaction forces and rate of force
development of any conventional barbell exercise. Not suitable for all populations
(technical complexity is high), but irreplaceable for athletes prioritising RFD
development.
- Compensatory Acceleration Training (CAT): Sub-maximal loads (50–70% 1RM)
moved with maximal voluntary acceleration intent throughout the entire concentric
phase. The Westside Barbell 'Dynamic Effort' method is the most systematised
application. CAT develops the neural drive for high RFD while managing the fatigue cost
of maximal loading. Evidence: Compensatory acceleration at 55% 1RM activates the
same high-threshold motor units as 85–90% 1RM loads when performed with maximal
intent (Duchateau & Hainaut, 1984).
- Heavy isometric overcoming contractions: Maximal-intent pushes or pulls against an
immovable object (pins, safety bars) at specific joint angles. Post-activation potentiation
from heavy isometrics can amplify dynamic force output by 5–10% for 5–12 minutes
post-stimulus — making them a powerful priming tool before maximal attempts. Also one
of the highest-RFD training stimuli available, as the nervous system fires maximally
without the velocity constraints of free weights.

-  Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP): Priming Your Nervous
System for Peak Output
Post-Activation Potentiation is one of the most practically powerful and chronically underused
tools in strength sport. The mechanism: a heavy conditioning stimulus (typically a maximal or
near-maximal effort on a movement similar to the target exercise) causes myosin regulatory
light chain phosphorylation and increased motor neuron excitability that transiently enhances
contractile force and RFD in subsequent efforts — even at sub-maximal loads.
The cellular mechanism: the heavy conditioning stimulus phosphorylates myosin regulatory light
chains via myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) activation. This phosphorylation increases the
sensitivity of the myosin head to calcium — so the same calcium transient produces greater
cross-bridge formation and faster force development. The effect peaks 5–12 minutes post-
stimulus and can persist for up to 30 minutes in well-trained individuals.

## 5.  The Central Governor & Psychological Priming: Hacking
## Your Inhibitory System
Tim Noakes' Central Governor Model proposes that the brain acts as a subconscious regulator
of athletic performance — limiting effort based on predictive models of danger rather than actual
physiological failure. In the strength context, this manifests as the inhibitory circuits described in
Section 2: the brain does not allow full motor unit recruitment because it is protecting against
catastrophic structural failure.
The practical implication is profound: your true physiological maximum is significantly above
your daily training maximum — because the central governor is always operating below the true
physical limit. The strategies that most effectively raise the ceiling of voluntary force output do
so by temporarily reducing central inhibition — not by increasing muscle size.
Evidence-Based Psychological Priming Techniques
- Attentional focus — external over internal: Wulf & Prinz's OPTIMAL theory and
subsequent EMG research: focusing on the movement outcome ('push the floor away')

- Controlled aggression (psyching up): Pre-lift arousal manipulations — forceful

elite powerlifters show measurable increases in prefrontal cortex activation and
decreased inhibitory interneuron activity in the 30 seconds before a maximal attempt.
The effect is real. The dose matters: over-arousal (panic, dissociated fear) impairs
technique and increases injury risk. The optimal state is controlled intensity — high
sympathetic activation with maintained technical focus.
- Ammonia inhalants: Stimulate the trigeminal nerve and vagal irritant receptors,
triggering a reflex sympathetic activation and brief cortical arousal spike. The effect on
voluntary force output is real and acute — studies show 3–8% increases in isometric
strength immediately following ammonia inhalation. Used situationally in powerlifting
competition or testing maxima. Not a training tool for regular sessions — the habituation
effect is rapid and the intensity of the stimulus is not appropriate for sub-maximal
training.
- Imagery and mental rehearsal: Motor imagery activates the same corticospinal
pathways as physical execution — fMRI and TMS studies confirm this consistently. Elite
strength athletes who perform 5–10 minutes of vivid kinesthetic imagery (feeling the lift,
not just visualising it externally) before competition attempts show measurably increased
cortical motor map excitability. The practical protocol: lying flat, eyes closed, 5 minutes
of first-person kinesthetic rehearsal of the target lift, including the setup, the descent, the
sticking point, and the successful lockout. Not mysticism — applied neuroscience.
- Verbal encouragement and social facilitation: Zajonc's social facilitation theory and
its applications to strength: the presence of an audience or a training partner who
provides forceful verbal encouragement during a maximal attempt increases voluntary
force output by 5–10% in trained athletes. The mechanism is cortical arousal
amplification. This is one of the most underappreciated reasons why gym PRs happen
with training partners and competition PRs happen in competition — the social context is
a genuine physiological input.

-  Biotensegrity & The Kinetic Chain: Why Your 'Weak Point'
## Is Almost Never Where You Think
The body does not produce force in isolated segments. It transmits force through a continuous
system of muscles, fascia, tendons, and bones — what biomechanists call the kinetic chain, and
what structural biologists increasingly model through the framework of biotensegrity: the body
as a tension-integrated structure where no element operates independently of the others.
The practical implication for strength training is critical and chronically misdiagnosed: a strength
weakness in a major movement is rarely caused by weakness in the obvious prime mover. It is
almost always caused by a 'power leak' — a point in the kinetic chain where force transmission
is lost because a stabilising structure is insufficiently stiff or insufficiently coordinated.

-  Velocity-Based Training: Real-Time Force-Velocity
## Profiling
Velocity-Based Training (VBT) represents the most significant methodological advancement in
strength training programming of the last two decades. The foundational principle: bar velocity is
a reliable, real-time proxy for relative intensity (%1RM) — because the force-velocity
relationship in muscle is predictable and individual-specific. If you know your mean concentric
velocity at a given load, you can infer your proximity to 1RM with high accuracy — without ever
performing a true maximum test.

Velocity Loss as a Fatigue Threshold
One of VBT's most powerful applications is using intra-set velocity loss as an autoregulated
stopping criterion. Research by Gonzalez-Badillo and colleagues established that velocity loss
within a set correlates predictably with neuromuscular fatigue accumulation. Two practical
thresholds:
- 10–15% velocity loss from first rep: Minimal fatigue accumulation. Optimal for
strength and power development. Maintains neural quality across the set.
Recommended for strength-speed and maximum strength zones.

- 20–25% velocity loss: Moderate fatigue. Acceptable for hypertrophy-focused blocks.

- 30%+ velocity loss: High fatigue accumulation. Appropriate for specific hypertrophy

Without a VBT device, the practical proxy is bar speed perception: if the bar begins to feel
'grinding' and you can perceive significant velocity loss from your first rep, that is your stopping
signal for strength sessions. Reserve grinding for the final set of hypertrophy-focused work only.

-  Periodisation for Strength: Peaking Without Leaving
Gains on the Table
Strength peaking — arriving at a competition or testing day at maximum expression of
accumulated adaptation — is a distinct skill from building strength over time. The majority of
intermediate lifters who hit a strength plateau are not limited by muscle mass or technical
efficiency. They are limited by fatigue masking their true strength — accumulated training stress
that suppresses performance below the adaptation level that has actually been built.
## The Supercompensation Model
After a training stimulus, performance temporarily drops (fatigue phase) before rising above the
pre-training baseline (supercompensation phase) before returning to baseline if no further
stimulus is applied (involution). The entire art of strength peaking is timing the deload to remove
fatigue at the precise moment when supercompensation peaks — exposing the full strength that
has been built but was previously masked by accumulated fatigue.
The Conjugate / Concurrent Model (Westside-Inspired)
The conjugate method, systematised by Louie Simmons at Westside Barbell, trains multiple
strength qualities simultaneously across the week — maximum effort work (ME), dynamic effort
work (DE), and repetition effort work (RE) — rather than sequencing them in discrete blocks.
The theoretical advantage: no quality is neglected for extended periods, reducing the detraining
of secondary qualities during single-focus blocks.
- Max Effort (ME) day: Work up to a maximal single, double, or triple in a variation of the
competition lift (box squat, floor press, rack pull). Rotated every 1–3 weeks to prevent
accommodation. Primary driver of maximal strength and motor unit synchronisation.
- Dynamic Effort (DE) day: 8–12 sets of 2–3 reps at 50–65% 1RM with accommodating
resistance (bands or chains), maximal compensatory acceleration intent. Develops RFD,
bar speed, and explosiveness in the competition movement patterns. Primary driver of
rate coding and neural efficiency.
- Repetition Effort (RE) work: Accessory movements taken to near-failure in moderate
rep ranges (8–15). Addresses hypertrophy for weak-point muscles identified in the

## OBJECTION CRUSHER — FAQ
Q1: How do weightlifters generate so much power without looking as big as
bodybuilders?
The power-to-size advantage of Olympic weightlifters is the clearest demonstration in sport that
strength and size are governed by different mechanisms. Elite weightlifters optimise three
variables simultaneously that bodybuilders do not: (1) Rate coding — their alpha motor neurons
fire at significantly higher frequencies due to years of high-velocity, high-force training,
producing greater temporal summation of calcium transients and therefore more force per unit
of muscle cross-section; (2) RFD — their entire training is built around producing peak force in
150–200 milliseconds (the catch phase of a clean or snatch), compared to the 400–500ms time
course of a bodybuilding rep; (3) Intermuscular coordination — the triple extension sequence
(ankle, knee, hip) in an Olympic lift requires extraordinarily precise multi-joint coordination,

maximising mechanical efficiency. A bodybuilder's muscle cross-section may produce more
peak force in a static test. But in any explosive movement with a real-world time constraint, the
weightlifter's superior RFD and neural efficiency produce dramatically greater power.
COMMAND: Add velocity-focused work (jump squats, power cleans, or CAT sets at 50–65%
1RM) to 1–2 sessions per week for 8 weeks. Measure vertical jump or 20m sprint before and
after. The RFD improvement is measurable and transfers.

Q2: Is a dirty bulk necessary to maximise strength, or can I stay lean?
A caloric surplus is mechanistically advantageous for maximal strength development for two
specific reasons: (1) mTORC1 is more permissive in a surplus — anabolic signalling efficiency
is higher when AMPK is not chronically elevated by caloric restriction; (2) glycogen stores are
maintained more easily in a surplus, reducing the risk of carbohydrate-limited CNS fatigue
during heavy training sessions. However, the difference between a modest surplus (+200–300
kcal/day, protein-forward) and an aggressive surplus ('dirty bulk' +800+ kcal/day) is negligible
for strength — the additional body fat produced by aggressive surplus does not convert to
additional strength, and the increasing body weight raises the load demands on your joints and
connective tissue without proportional strength benefit. The optimal approach for strength: a
modest surplus (200–400 kcal) during accumulation blocks; maintenance during intensification
and peaking blocks. Stay within 3–5% body fat of your competition or target weight year-round.
COMMAND: Run a 200–300 kcal surplus (protein-prioritised) during accumulation and
hypertrophy blocks. Cut to maintenance 4–6 weeks before a strength test or competition peak.
Do not deliberately gain body fat in the name of strength — the evidence does not support the
exchange rate.

Q3: How many times a week can I max out before I fry my CNS?
True 1RM attempts (maximum singles with maximal psychological priming) generate the
highest CNS fatigue load of any training action — requiring 48–96 hours for full neural recovery
in well-trained athletes. Evidence from Bulgarian method practitioners (who peaked at 6 training
days per week with daily maximal singles) suggests elite athletes with years of adaptation can
tolerate higher frequency max-effort work. But this is not a starting point — it is the endpoint of
years of progressive frequency loading. For intermediate lifters, the evidence-based
recommendation is 1–2 true maximal attempts per week per movement, supplemented by sub-
maximal work (90–95% for sets of 2–3) at higher frequency. The practical signal that CNS
fatigue is limiting your maxing frequency: bar speed at 85% 1RM is noticeably slower than your
baseline velocity profile, subjective motivation is low, and technical errors increase in patterns
not explained by load.
COMMAND: Limit true max singles (100% 1RM effort) to once per week per movement. Work
in the 87.5–95% range for 1–3 reps on 1–2 additional sessions. Track bar velocity — if 85%
feels like 92%, the CNS needs a deload, not another max day.

Q4: My squat is weak out of the hole — is it my quads, my hips, or my nervous
system?
Failure out of the hole in a squat (0–90 degrees of knee flexion) is almost always a
convergence of two factors, rarely just one: (1) Quad strength in the deep knee flexion position
— the vastus medialis and intermedius are at a significant mechanical disadvantage in the

deepest range of the squat, where the moment arm is longest and the joint angle least efficient.
Pause squats at the bottom position and leg press with slow eccentrics specifically develop this
range. (2) Hip adductor stability — the adductors function as hip extensors when the hip is at
end-range flexion (the bottom of a squat). Weakness here manifests as knee cave and a
collapse of the bottom position rather than pure quad failure. Copenhagen planks and supine
adductor squeeze assessments will diagnose this. The neural contribution: if the pattern only
fails under psychological pressure (competition, heavy loads, when fatigue accumulates) but
not in isolation, the deficit is partially in neural drive and partially in PAP optimisation of the
warm-up sequence.
COMMAND: Add 3x/week: pause squats (3-sec pause at the bottom) for 4 sets of 4–6 reps at
60–65% 1RM + Copenhagen adductor plank holds 3x30 seconds. Reassess after 8 weeks.
The combination addresses both the quad strength-in-range deficit and the adductor stability
power leak simultaneously.

Q5: What is the best periodisation model to peak for a powerlifting meet?
The evidence most consistently supports block periodisation for meet preparation, with three
distinct phases as detailed in Section 8: accumulation (volume-dominant, 70–80%),
intensification (intensity-dominant, 80–90%), and realisation (competition-specific, 87.5–
97.5%). The key variables that separate a successful meet peak from a failed one are: (1) The
deload timing — the final deload must be complete 5–7 days before competition, not 10–14
days (too long, supercompensation dissipates) and not 2–3 days (too short, fatigue not
cleared); (2) Opener selection — first attempts should be something you could do on your worst
day, having just been woken from sleep: typically 90–92% of your best recent training lift; (3)
The meet warm-up — mirror your training warm-up protocol precisely, including PAP primer if
you use one in training. Competition is not the time to experiment with any variable that differs
from your training practice.
COMMAND: Run the 12-week block periodisation template from Section 8. Set openers at 90–
92% of best training lift. Execute the same PAP and priming protocol you used in the final
weeks of training. Trust the preparation.
